Background


  • SEER is among the largest cancer data collection programs in the world, and covers about 28 percent of the US population.

  • In the past few years, SEER has released 2010 to 2013 data on brain metastases diagnosed at the time of primary cancer diagnosis (called “synchronous” brain metastases).

  • While synchronous brain metastases are very informative, the lifetime frequencies of BM remain less well characterized.


  • Consequently, the first aim of this work is to:
    1. Validate Medicare claims against SEER-reported BM and
    2. Describe the frequency of lifetime BM diagnoses in the Medicare population.


  • Knowing which subjects were diagnosed with CNS metastases, and having detailed treatment records available in the form of Medicare inpatient, outpatient, and prescription claims, the next aim is to:
    1. Identify differences in BM frequency across primary cancer treatments and
    2. Describe BM treatment patterns across demographic and primary cancer characteristics.

Methods



Noteworthy issue:




SEER does not have confidence in the use of claims data to identify secondary cancer diagnoses, though their references are mostly at least a decade old.


Our work examining metastases has the benefit of:

  1. Using diagnosis codes in conjunction with procedure codes and
  2. Comparing these diagnostic criteria compare to recently-released SEER data on brain metastases

Medicare claims caveats



Diagnoses and dates of diagnoses
  • We will be examining the date of diagnosis closest to primary cancer diagnosis because SEER only provides data on “synchronous” brain metastases, which are those brain metastases diagnosed at the same time as the primary cancer
  • These were searched for reference to a diagnosis code of 198.3X (CNS metastasis).
  • The date of Medicare CNS mets diagnosis was compared to the date of primary cancer diagnosis to yield a number of days between primary and secondary CNS metastasis diagnosis, of which the minimum was taken to be the date of Medicare diagnosis.
  • Procedure and treatment codes are currently being extracted


    Available years
  • We have Medicare claims data for subjects diagnosed in 2008 through 2012
  • SEER “synchronous” mets data is provided for the years 2010-2013
  • Claims data was not available for the diagnosis year 2013 because we would need claims through 2015 to ensure sufficient coverage of patient care

CNS Mets diagnosis - SEER vs Medicare


SEER definition/description of their “brain metastases” variable:

Identifies the presence of distant metastatic involvement of the brain at the time of diagnosis.

The presence of metastatic brain disease at diagnosis is an independent prognostic indicator, and it is used by Collaborative Staging to derive TNM-M codes and SEER Summary Stage codes for some sites.

This field should be coded for all solid tumors, Kaposi sarcoma, Unknown Primary Site, and Other and Ill-Defined Primary Sites.

Only available for 2010+ diagnosis.

Note: This includes only the brain, not spinal cord or other parts of the central nervous system.


The following few definitions served to produce this report:

  1. Lifetime diagnoses - Presence of an ICD9 code corresponding to secondary CNS cancer (198.3X)
  2. 30-day diagnoses - Presence of an ICD9 code corresponding to secondary CNS cancer (198.3X) within 30 days of primary cancer diagnosis
  3. 90-day diagnoses - Presence of an ICD9 code corresponding to secondary CNS cancer (198.3X) within 90 days of primary cancer diagnosis

    The ICD diagnosis code only reaches a central nervous system-level description of metastases, but SEER specifically reports brain metastases. So, in addition to the CNS diagnosis code, we’ll also include intracranial procedure codes…

Appendix I: Criteria notes

  1. n : This is the total number of records available from Medicare
  2. HMO Use : Used HMO HMO use would imply a lack of reporting to Medicare, which would also mean it is inappropriate to include that subject in this work
  3. Age : Under 65 years We need people who use Medicare due only to age, this helps keep the population homogenous
  4. Diagnosis method : Autopsy/Death Certificate Primary cancer diagnosis on autopsy or death certificate would mean that a person wasn’t followed up by Medicare for their disease, or their disease was not clinically evident
  5. Medicare eligibility : ESRD only We exclude people who are eligible due to ESRD only because their primary cancer disease process is unlike that of a subject without ESRD.
  6. Medicare eligibility : Aged with ESRD This is the same as number 5, substituting the relevant eligibility value
  7. Medicare eligibility : Disabled This is the same as number 5, substituting the relevant eligibility value
  8. Medicare eligibility : Disabled with ESRD This is the same as number 5, substituting the relevant eligibility value
  9. Race : Unknown I’ve seen this in other papers, but I would guess it’s because subjects whose race is not known are not followed up as well
  10. Survival Time (Months) : 9999 This value indicates a missing survival time, which doesn’t signal such great follow up
  11. Death Verified : No Again, this is a signal of not-ideal follow-up
  12. Insurance Payer : Insurance status unknown We’re only looking at people who used Medicare
  13. Insurance Payer : Not insured We’re only looking at people who used Medicare
  14. Heterogenous primaries : Unaccounted primaries present Each patient in SEER has a “sequence number” indicating which of the patient’s primary diagnoses that record represents. If a patient had a greater sequence number than they did records of breast/lung/skin cancer, then they were excluded from this analysis.
